# Sunk Cost Fallacy

**We continue investing time, money, or effort into something because of what we have already spent — even when the rational choice is to stop.**

## How it works

A sunk cost is any investment that cannot be recovered: money already spent, time already used, effort already exerted. Rational decision theory says these costs are irrelevant to future decisions — only future costs and benefits matter.

But we find it psychologically painful to "waste" past investments, so we stay on failing courses of action to justify them. The longer and more painful the prior investment, the stronger the pull to continue. This can turn a small loss into a catastrophic one.

## Everyday examples

- **Finishing a bad film:** "I've already watched an hour, I might as well see how it ends" — but the hour is gone regardless; the only question is whether the next hour is worth spending.
- **Failing projects:** A company continues funding a struggling product line because of the millions already invested, rather than reallocating resources to more promising alternatives.
- **Unhealthy relationships:** Staying in a situation that is clearly not working "because of everything we've been through together."
- **Education:** Completing a degree in a field you no longer want to work in, because you've already paid two years of tuition.

## How to counter it

- **Ask the "fresh start" question** — "If I had not already spent X, would I choose to start this now?" If the answer is no, the sunk cost is driving the decision.
- **Focus only on future costs and benefits** — explicitly exclude prior investments from your analysis.
- **Name it** — simply recognising "I'm about to commit the sunk cost fallacy" is often enough to interrupt the pattern.
- **Separate identity from investment** — the decision to stop is not an admission of failure; it is a rational response to new information.
