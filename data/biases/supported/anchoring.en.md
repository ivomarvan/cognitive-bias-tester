# Anchoring Bias

**We rely too heavily on the first piece of information we encounter when making a decision — even when that information is irrelevant.**

## How it works

When we receive an initial number or estimate, it becomes a mental "anchor." All subsequent judgments are made relative to this anchor, not from a neutral starting point. The anchor sticks even when we consciously try to ignore it, and even when we know it was chosen arbitrarily.

The effect is strongest when the question is uncertain and the anchor is the first piece of data we receive.

## Everyday examples

- **Salary negotiation:** Whoever names a number first sets the anchor. A high opening offer pulls the final agreement upward.
- **Retail pricing:** "Was €200, now €120" — the crossed-out price is an anchor that makes €120 feel like a bargain, regardless of actual value.
- **Estimating project time:** The first estimate a team hears tends to survive, even after new information emerges.
- **Medical diagnosis:** A doctor's initial hypothesis can anchor subsequent interpretation of test results.

## How to counter it

- **Form your own estimate first** — before asking for or receiving any reference number.
- **Ask "what would I think without this anchor?"** — consciously consider the question as if you had seen no prior number.
- **Seek multiple independent references** — diversifying anchor sources reduces the pull of any single one.
- **Slow down** — anchoring is stronger under time pressure; deliberate thinking weakens it.
