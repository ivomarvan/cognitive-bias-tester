# Loss Aversion

**Losing something hurts roughly twice as much as gaining the equivalent feels good.**

## How it works

Loss aversion is not simply being cautious — it is a systematic asymmetry in how our brains weight gains and losses. Research by Kahneman and Tversky found that the psychological pain of losing €100 is approximately double the pleasure of gaining €100.

This asymmetry evolved because avoiding harm had greater survival value than acquiring benefits. In modern life, it leads us to hold on to losing positions, refuse fair bets, and make conservative choices that cost us more in the long run.

## Everyday examples

- **Investing:** Holding a losing stock for months hoping to "break even," even when the rational choice is to sell and reinvest.
- **Insurance:** Paying premiums far above the expected value of a claim, to avoid the pain of an uninsured loss.
- **Job changes:** Staying in an unfulfilling job because leaving feels like "losing" security, even when a new opportunity offers better expected outcomes.
- **Negotiations:** Feeling more pressure to avoid conceding a point than to gain an equivalent concession from the other side.

## How to counter it

- **Calculate expected value** — ignore the gain/loss frame; ask "what is the probability-weighted outcome?"
- **Ask the reversal question** — "If I didn't already own this, would I pay the current price to acquire it?" (The endowment effect is closely related.)
- **Distinguish real risk from discomfort** — ask whether the "loss" is an actual harm or just the discomfort of change.
- **Set decision rules in advance** — pre-commit stop-loss thresholds before entering a position, to remove the in-the-moment emotional calculation.
