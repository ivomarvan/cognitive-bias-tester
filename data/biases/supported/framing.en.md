# Framing Effect

**The way information is presented changes our decision — even when the underlying facts are identical.**

## How it works

Our minds don't evaluate options in isolation; we evaluate them relative to how they're described. A choice framed as a gain triggers a different emotional response than the same choice framed as a loss. Because emotions influence decisions faster than rational analysis, the frame often wins before logic gets a chance.

The effect is particularly strong for risk: we tend to be risk-averse when a situation is framed positively ("saves lives") and risk-seeking when the same situation is framed negatively ("lives lost").

## Everyday examples

- **Medical statistics:** "90% survival rate" and "10% mortality rate" are identical facts — but patients prefer treatments described with the survival frame.
- **Food labels:** "10% fat" sounds worse than "90% fat-free," even though they mean the same thing.
- **Policy debates:** "Reducing the budget deficit" and "cutting public services" can describe the same policy, but trigger opposite reactions.
- **Investment:** "Lock in a guaranteed gain of €500" versus "avoid a certain loss of €500" — same outcome, opposite framing.

## How to counter it

- **Restate the problem in the opposite frame** — if it's presented as a gain, reframe it as a loss, and vice versa. If your answer changes, a frame is influencing you.
- **Focus on absolute numbers** — convert percentages and relative comparisons to absolute values to strip away framing.
- **Separate description from decision** — notice the emotional tone of how something is presented before evaluating the substance.
