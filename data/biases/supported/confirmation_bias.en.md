# Confirmation Bias

**We seek, interpret, and remember information in a way that confirms what we already believe.**

## How it works

When we hold a belief or hypothesis, we unconsciously filter incoming information: we notice and remember evidence that supports the belief, and we downplay, forget, or explain away evidence that contradicts it. The result is that our confidence grows without our beliefs actually being tested.

Confirmation bias operates at every stage of reasoning: in how we search for information, how we evaluate it, and how we recall it later. It is amplified by social media algorithms, which show us content we are likely to agree with.

## Everyday examples

- **Medical self-diagnosis:** Reading about a disease online, we notice every symptom that matches our suspected condition and ignore those that don't fit.
- **Performance reviews:** A manager who has formed a positive impression of an employee will interpret ambiguous actions as competence; a manager with a negative impression will interpret the same actions as incompetence.
- **Politics:** People read primarily news sources that align with their views and remember studies that confirm their preferred policies.
- **Hiring:** An interviewer who likes a candidate early in the interview subconsciously asks easier questions and interprets answers more generously.

## How to counter it

- **Actively seek disconfirming evidence** — explicitly ask "what would prove me wrong?" and then look for it.
- **Steel-man the opposing view** — before dismissing a contrary argument, build the strongest possible version of it.
- **Use pre-mortems** — before committing to a decision, imagine it has already failed and list the reasons why.
- **Separate data collection from interpretation** — record observations before forming conclusions, to avoid contaminating raw data with prior beliefs.
